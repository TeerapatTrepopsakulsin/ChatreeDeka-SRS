This section examines the two existing Thai legal AI platforms most directly relevant to
ChatreeDeka: KHORN AI and ThanoyAI. The analysis focuses on each platform's technical
architecture, functional scope, and capability boundaries, as these determine where
ChatreeDeka's design diverges from prior work.

\subsubsection{KHORN AI}

KHORN AI is a legal research platform targeted at law firms and legal practitioners. Its
core function is a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline that indexes approximately
120,000 Thai Supreme Court rulings and 1,400 statutory texts. The system exposes a semantic
search interface that accepts natural-language queries and returns ranked, cited passages
from that corpus.

Functional capabilities include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Semantic intent parsing over a private vector database, enabling cross-document
    similarity matching between a submitted fact pattern and precedent case holdings.

    \item A ``Deep Research'' mode that performs multi-hop retrieval across statutes,
    supporting evidence mapping between a legal claim and the relevant code provisions.

    \item Zero-retention data handling via a private vector store, relevant to
    confidentiality obligations in a law-firm context.

    \item Single-turn query model: the system responds to a posed question from retrieved
    context but does not maintain session state or model argument progression across
    multiple turns.

    \item No adversarial counterpart agent: the system does not simulate opposing counsel
    or generate counter-arguments against a submitted position.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{ThanoyAI}

ThanoyAI is a legal question-answering service delivered through the LINE messaging
platform. Its architecture is oriented toward high-throughput, low-latency responses in a
messaging context. The system processes short factual queries and returns brief answers
with statutory citations.

Functional capabilities include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Single-turn query-response pattern within the LINE API, optimised for direct
    factual answers with cited statutory references.

    \item Narrow functional scope limited to statutory Q\&A; the system does not model
    case progression, maintain session state, or reason about opposing arguments.

    \item No agent-based reasoning: responses are generated from prompt-completion over
    a retrieval layer; no component is present that models adversarial legal strategy.

    \item No post-session analysis: the system produces no outcome estimation,
    win-probability scoring, or post-interaction feedback.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Capability Gaps and Design Rationale}

The comparison identifies two structural absences in the current Thai legal AI landscape
that directly inform ChatreeDeka's architecture.

\textbf{Absence of multi-turn adversarial simulation.} Both platforms operate in a
stateless, single-turn mode. Neither maintains session context across a trial progression,
and neither incorporates an agent representing an opposing position. This limits their
utility for evaluating argument durability under sustained cross-examination or when
additional evidence is introduced during a proceeding. A practitioner using either
platform can retrieve relevant precedents but cannot observe how those precedents perform
when contested.

\textbf{Absence of probabilistic outcome estimation.} Neither platform produces a
quantitative estimate of trial outcome. The Case Predictor Agent in ChatreeDeka is
designed to address this by scoring win probability against historical Deka verdicts drawn
from the RAG knowledge base. The reliability of this estimate depends on the coverage and
quality of the underlying corpus, which is scoped in Section~3.x.

These gaps reflect deliberate design trade-offs rather than deficiencies: both platforms
optimise for retrieval depth and response latency within their respective deployment
contexts. ChatreeDeka is designed to complement document-centric research tools.
Practitioners can use platforms such as KHORN AI to identify relevant precedents, then
apply ChatreeDeka to evaluate how those precedents hold up under simulated adversarial
examination.

This section examines recent multi-agent frameworks in legal AI and explains how their methodologies inform the architectural and technical decisions of the \textbf{ChatreeDeka} platform.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Judicial Simulation (AgentsCourt)}
He et al. (2024) proposed \textbf{AgentsCourt} \cite{arxiv_2403_02959}, a framework that simulates complete court trials using Large Language Models (LLMs). It employs role-playing agents (judge, plaintiff, defendant) and a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) system to reconstruct court proceedings and retrieve relevant legal documents. While it achieved significant improvements in legal text generation accuracy, it relies heavily on static historical judgments and prompt engineering, which limits its capacity for dynamic, adversarial reasoning.

\textbf{Influence on System Design:} AgentsCourt validates our architectural choice to divide the simulation into distinct, role-specific entities (Judge, Prosecutor, Defender). However, its limitations in handling dynamic courtroom unpredictability drove our decision to move beyond pure LLM prompting and incorporate a more strategic training methodology for our opponent agents.

\subsection{Adversarial Legal Simulation (LegalSim)}
Badhe et al. (2025) introduced \textbf{LegalSim} \cite{arxiv_2510_03405}, a framework that treats litigation as a strategic interaction under codified rules. Using a rules-as-code engine and Reinforcement Learning (specifically PPO), the framework trains agents to discover procedural vulnerabilities and optimize legal strategies (e.g., applying settlement pressure or calendar pressure). LegalSim proved that RL-trained agents can uncover emergent, complex legal tactics that simple heuristic or LLM-based policies miss.

\textbf{Influence on System Design:} LegalSim provides the foundational justification for utilizing Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) to train the \textbf{ChatreeDeka} Defender Agent. By optimizing the agent's policy against the rules of the Thai Civil and Commercial Code, we ensure it generates realistic, strategically robust counter-arguments and cross-examinations rather than merely predicting plausible conversational text.

\subsection{Synthesis and System Justification}
Current research reveals a dichotomy between faithful historical simulation (AgentsCourt) and dynamic strategic optimization (LegalSim). \textbf{ChatreeDeka} is designed to bridge this gap. By combining the conversational realism of LLM role-play with the strategic robustness of MARL, our system provides a highly dynamic trial environment. This synthesized approach ensures that our Case Predictor Agent evaluates win probabilities based on both accurate statutory interpretation and realistic adversarial pressure.
